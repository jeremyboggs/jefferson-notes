In ink, Jefferson transcribes the printed text from the 1787 printed edition up until "a violent head-ach," (The hand employed here, extremely neat and tiny, may produce a headache in some readers.) A caret, in pencil, is matched by a second caret following "the fissure," and echoed in the printed text in a corresponding position. Jefferson continues, "this painful sensation [...]". He scratches out and corrects three words, inserting "three eights of a mile."

On page 35, visible in the scan, a pencil crosses out "If the view from the top be pain-" and the cancellation continues on page 36, where the strike through ends at "equal extreme." A strike-through, in pencil, cancels twelve lines, ending and bracketed at the phrase "about five miles." Jefferson's revision corrects an inaccuracy of memory, described on the leaf's verso. Compare the 1853 edition, which includes different language:

and descending then to the valley below, the sensation becomes delightful in the extreme. It is impossible for the emotions arising from the sublime to be felt beyond what they are here: so beautiful an arch, so elevated, so light, and springing as it were up to heaven, the rapture of the spectator is really indescribable. The fissure continues deep and narrow, and following the margin of the stream upwards, about three-eights of a mile, you arrive at a limestone cavern, less remarkable for the height and extent than those before described. Its entrance into the hill is but a few feet above the bed of the stream. This bridge is in the county of Rockbridge, to which it has given name, and affords a public and commodious passage over a valley, which cannot be crossed elsewhere for a considerable distance. The stream passing under it is called Cedar Creek. It is a water of James River, and sufficient in the dryest seasons to turn a grist mill, though its fountain is not more than two miles above.
